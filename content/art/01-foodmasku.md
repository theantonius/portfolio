foodmasku is an ongoing performance, photography, and video series in which I make meals into masks and then eat them. It began in April 2020, during the early weeks of COVID lockdown, when a colleague's stuck pickle filter on a Zoom call prompted me to put kale on my face. It was the first I'd heard laughter in weeks. I felt it was important to find joy through art again. And so, a daily art practice was born.

Over the years the project has grown into a body of work at the intersection of trauma, food waste, and responsible consumption. The UN commissioned me to make a series on sustainable food systems, using the face as a site for meditating on how food reaches our tables, and what we choose to do with it when it arrives. Farm-to-Face was unveiled at the 2023 UN SDG Action Awards alongside the UN Food Systems Summit.

The series has also lived an unruly second life online. A performance staged at The Invisible Dog Art Center where two guests quietly eating dinner off my face was cut, recaptioned, and recirculated across TikTok and Instagram, racking up tens of millions of views and a segment on MTV's Ridiculousness. The runaway clip is frequently paired with fabricated captions about a "restaurant paying $10,000 a month for rich people to eat off your face," a viral rumor I continue to publicly debunk. The real piece is about labor, food inequality, and who gets to be seen at the table.
